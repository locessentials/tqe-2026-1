Copyright and Artificial Intelligence

January 9, 2025

By: Ricardo Arturo Gómez Hernández and Daniel Ascencio Rodríguez

The rise of artificial intelligence (“AI”) is driving fundamental shifts in how we live, both socially and privately. As these changes accelerate, legislation and the institutions that govern our conduct must adapt. One area already experiencing change—albeit in its early stages—is the production of creative works. This article focuses specifically on copyright issues arising from the use of AI in the creation of such works.

Before delving into the core topic, it is essential to introduce the Dark Forest Theory of the Internet. Its central premise is that the internet is increasingly overrun by bots and AI-generated content. As a result, “real people” are retreating into closed, walled-off spaces where barriers to entry are high enough to keep non-human agents out (Appleton, 2023). This trend is now spilling over into other areas of human activity, including the creation of works—particularly in literature. Several years ago, a novel written with the assistance of AI tools was entered into a literary contest in Japan and even passed the initial screening (Olewitz, 2016). This milestone illustrates how AI is becoming ever more capable of passing as human, while also raising fundamental questions about copyright: starting with whether such a novel is even eligible for copyright protection, and if so, who should own the rights.

Unsurprisingly, the answer depends on the applicable legal framework. Accordingly, this article will (i) outline current Mexican copyright law as it applies to works created with the assistance of AI tools, and (ii) propose, in broad strokes, measures to strengthen the Mexican institutions responsible for addressing these challenges.

**I. Current Mexican Copyright Framework for Works Created with AI Assistance**

Under Mexico’s Federal Copyright Law (*Ley Federal del Derecho de Autor*, “LFDA”), Article 12 provides that authorship—i.e., the status of being the creator of a literary or artistic work—is recognized exclusively for natural persons (and, by a *contrario sensu* interpretation, not for legal entities). Under current Mexican law, large language models (“LLMs”) and other AI technologies are not recognized as having legal personality, and therefore cannot qualify as authors under the cited provision. Even if AI were to be granted legal personality, that alone would not suffice, as the law limits authorship to natural persons—unless the definition of natural person were itself amended to include AI. The rationale behind this restriction is to preserve creativity as a uniquely human attribute, grounded in an interpretation of Article 28, paragraph 11, of the Mexican Constitution.

What happens, then, when a natural person uses LLMs or other AI tools to create a work? To whom should rights be assigned? To the AI user? To the AI’s developer? To the copyright holders of the data used to train the AI? What problems does this create, and how might they be addressed? As Spindler (October 2024) aptly frames it, the core issue boils down to this: how are copyrights allocated for works created with AI tools? The answer hinges on determining the degree of AI involvement in the work and distinguishing it from human contribution.

Under Mexican law, while it is clear that AI cannot be considered an author due to the requirement that authors be natural persons (and, more generally, because AI lacks any form of legal personality), the LFDA does not resolve whether the user who inputs instructions or parameters into an AI system may be deemed the creator—assuming the user is indeed a human and not another AI, which would raise a separate set of legal questions not addressed here. Nor does the law clarify the extent of such a user’s copyright interest in the resulting work.

**II. Proposal to Strengthen Mexican Institutions Charged with Copyright Registration**

Alan Turing originally proposed a test designed to determine, based solely on written responses, whether the subject was a human or a machine (Oppy & Dowe, 2021). If a machine succeeded in deceiving the observer into believing it was human, it would pass the test. Today, AI—particularly LLMs—passes the Turing Test with increasing ease, which has spurred the development of tools designed to distinguish AI-generated outputs from human creations. One such tool is the Reverse Turing Test (“RTT”), in which a machine is tasked with distinguishing between humans and machines—the inverse of the traditional Turing Test. A common application of the RTT is the CAPTCHA system, which uses text and images to determine whether a website user is human. Yet even CAPTCHA is becoming less effective at distinguishing human users from bots on social media, where automated accounts are rampant (Alizadeh et. al., 2021).

Given the legislative gap described above and the availability of technological tools to distinguish human-authored works from AI-generated ones, it is important to recognize the fundamentally protective nature of Mexico’s copyright system—particularly with respect to authors’ moral rights. We believe that the way forward lies in returning to the roots of the Mexican copyright system. In other words, one of the principal practical challenges posed by AI is not so much devising rules for assigning authorship to non-human entities, but rather the ease with which this technology enables users to plagiarize or otherwise misappropriate the artistic or intellectual creations of others. Recognizing this brings us closer to a workable solution.

Our core proposal is therefore to strengthen Mexico’s copyright system by reinforcing the institutions responsible for its structure and safeguards, equipping them with the tools to anticipate the wave of challenges that new AI technologies will bring. Specifically, we propose two concrete measures: (a) introduce a reliable Reverse Turing Test as a prerequisite for registering a work with the Copyright Registry—such a filter could potentially be automated to avoid overwhelming the authorities while respecting the Registry’s good-faith character; and (b) establish an external consultation service for information on registered and protected works with the National Institute of Copyright (INDAUTOR), making it easier for copyright holders or prospective registrants to monitor their interests. These proposals aim to reduce the number of disputes that escalate into litigation over copyright matters—disputes that are likely to increase as the volume of creative works grows with the proliferation of LLMs and other AI tools.

Span Agreement — Exact Match, F1, Precision & Recall

Exact Match Rate — percentage of error spans where two or more annotators identified identical start and end character positions. Strictest measure of boundary agreement.

Precision — of all characters annotator A marked as errors, what proportion was also marked by annotator B. High precision means annotators rarely flag spans the other ignores.

Recall — of all characters annotator B marked as errors, what proportion was also marked by annotator A. High recall means annotators rarely miss spans the other caught.

F1 Score — harmonic mean of Precision and Recall, balancing the two into a single partial-overlap agreement score. Penalises large imbalances between precision and recall more than a simple average would. Unlike exact matching, partial credit is awarded when spans overlap, making F1 a more lenient measure of boundary agreement. Production deployment target for F1: ≥0.70.

Cohen's Kappa — Error Category Agreement

Pairwise Cohen's Kappa scores measuring agreement on error category labels between each pair of annotators, accounting for chance agreement. Cohen’s κ measures agreement on error category labels for overlapping spans. Production deployment target for both metrics: ≥0.60. Low reliability in one condition means its quality metrics should be interpreted with caution. Kappa values: <0.20 poor · 0.21–0.40 fair · 0.41–0.60 moderate · 0.61–0.80 substantial · >0.80 almost perfect.
